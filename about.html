Leonard Blaschek

Plant Physiology, Biochemistry and Genetics

PhD candidate in the Pesquet Lab at Stockholm University, Sweden

.

My research aims to explain how plants modulate wood properties from one cell type to another and on even smaller scales. As plant cells fulfill very different purposes, the composition and therfore the structural characteristics of their cell walls tend to vary widely between cell types. Lignin – from the latin lignum* for wood – is a complex, polyphenolic polymer that reinforces, waterproofs and protects the plant cell wall. How plants achieve the especially sharp gradients in lignin amount and composition on a sub-cellular scale is however still largely unknown.

I use genetic transformation of model plants coupled with biochemical and cytological analyses to investigate whether LACCASES, the proteins that are responsible for the polymerisation of lignin, have more specific roles than is widely assumed. Showing cell-type specific activity of different lacccases towards selected lignin building-blocks would significantly advance our understanding of how plants form the second most abundant biopolymer on earth. Moreover, it would enable cell specific manipulation of cell wall properties to design biomass that can be easily processed, reducing the need for chemical bleaching.

Plants fascinate, force perspectives unfamiliar to us mammals and are uniquely fundamental to life. When I am not investigating how plants work, I try to spend as much time as possible among them.
