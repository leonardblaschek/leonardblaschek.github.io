Leonard Blaschek

Plant Physiology, Biochemistry and Genetics
PhD candidate in the Pesquet Lab at Stockholm University, Sweden.

Plant physiology and morphology are dependent on a multitude of different specialised cell types. In all of them, the cell wall functions as an exoskeleton for each single cell and forms a cohesive interface between them. In general, the cell wall is a gel-like matrix composed of multiple bio-polymers including pectins, cellulose, hemicelluloses and, in woody cells, a polyphenolic called lignin. Along with their different functions, the composition and biochemical characteristics of plant cell walls vary widely between cell types. However, both the mechanism of how cell wall properties are modulated on the nanometre scale, and how these differences support the function of the respective cell, are still poorly understood.

I use genetic transformation of model plants coupled with biochemical and cytological analyses to investigate the regulation and effect of specific changes in lignin content in the cell wall. I am especially interested in whether LACCASES – proteins involved in the oxidative polymerisation of lignin – have more specific roles than is widely assumed and potentially direct lignin composition on the sub-cellular scale. Understanding the process of lignification on the cellular level would allow us to engineer plants that are more efficient renewable energy sources or sustainable building materials, whithout compromising plant growth.

When I am not working to undertand their inner workings, I spend as much of my time as possible hiking, climbing and canoeing among plants.
